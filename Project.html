Key Insights

Based on the analysis of the Heart Disease UCI dataset, here are the answers to the key questions:

1. Does cholesterol strongly impact heart disease?

  • Correlation Analysis: The correlation between cholesterol (chol) and heart disease (condition) is approximately 0.12, indicating a weak positive relationship. Higher cholesterol levels are associated with a slightly higher risk of heart disease, but it's not a strong predictor.
  • Visualization: The scatter plot of Age vs Cholesterol showed a moderate positive correlation (0.21), but cholesterol alone does not strongly determine disease presence.

2. Is male population more vulnerable?

  • Gender Analysis: Males (sex=1) have a higher average cholesterol (239.6) compared to females (sex=0, 261.3), but the disease rate is higher in males (32.3% of males vs 67.7% of females in the dataset).
  • Percentage Distribution: The dataset has 32.3% males and 67.7% females, with disease rates of 67.7% for males and 32.3% for females when normalized. Males appear more vulnerable based on the higher disease rate among them.

3. Does exercise-induced angina significantly increase risk?

  • Exang Analysis: Patients with exercise-induced angina (exang=1) have a disease rate of 76.4%, compared to 40.6% for those without (exang=0). This indicates a significant increase in risk.
  • Visualization: The bar chart shows a clear difference, with exang=1 having much higher disease probability.

4. Which feature has the strongest correlation with disease?

  • Correlation Matrix: From the correlation analysis, the features with the strongest correlations to condition are:
    • cp (chest pain type): 0.43
    • thalach (maximum heart rate): -0.42
    • exang (exercise-induced angina): 0.44
    • oldpeak (ST depression): 0.42
    • thal (thalassemia): 0.52
    • ca (number of major vessels): 0.46
  • Strongest: Thalassemia (thal) has the strongest correlation (0.52), followed by exercise-induced angina (0.44).

These insights are derived from the exploratory data analysis performed in this notebook. Participants can refer to the visualizations and statistics for deeper understanding.
